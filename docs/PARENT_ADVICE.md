# Parent Guide: Managing Maths Sprints & Supporting Your Child

This guide is designed to help you optimize your child's learning experience with the Maths Tutor application. It covers recommended sprint frequency, screen time guidelines, the mechanics of the adaptive learning system, and parent strategies for when children skip or struggle with questions.

---

## ⏱️ Recommended Weekly Sprint Schedule

For a 10-year-old child (Year 5 or 6), pedagogical research and platform standards (such as IXL and DoodleMaths) emphasize a **"little and often"** approach over long, infrequent cramming sessions.

| Aspect | Recommendation | Details |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Session Length** | **20 minutes per day** | The maximum recommended focus duration for a single daily session. |
| **Weekly Target** | **3 to 5 sprints per week** | **60 to 100 minutes** of total practice per week. |
| **Rest Days** | **2 to 3 days off per week** | Critical to prevent fatigue, support habit-forming, and keep practice enjoyable. |

> [!TIP]
> Spreading practice across 3 to 5 days is more effective for memory retention than doing one long 60-minute session on the weekend. Sleeping between learning sessions is when long-term memory consolidation happens!

## 🧠 Handling Skipping & "Speed-Running" Questions

It is common for children to submit random answers (or incorrect guesses twice) to bypass questions they don't understand. 

### Why Children Skip Questions
1. **Avoiding Friction / Anxiety:** Facing a problem they don't understand can trigger stress or math anxiety. Bypassing it relieves that pressure.
2. **Incentive Alignment:** If a high-value reward is tied strictly to **Sprints Completed** (like a Weekend TV reward), children learn that failing a question twice is the fastest way to progress the timer and complete the session.
3. **Fear of Failure:** Submitting a random guess can feel psychologically safer than making an honest effort and getting it wrong.

### How the System Responds
When a child gets a question wrong twice, the app classifies it as an incorrect response:
* **Difficulty Calibration:** The adaptive system will automatically **lower the difficulty level** for that topic (usually by 1 level) to help the child find their footing.
* **Mastery Impact:** The child's mastery level for that topic will decrease.
* **Earlier Review:** The topic will be scheduled for review sooner (usually tomorrow) to ensure they get another opportunity to practice.

> [!WARNING]
> If a child "speed-runs" a sprint by skipping everything, the system will continuously drop the difficulty. Eventually, the questions will become *too* easy, which can lead to boredom and a lack of progression.

## 🛠️ Actionable Parenting Strategies

### 1. Balance the Reward System (Process vs. Accuracy)
While rewarding **Sprints Completed** encourages consistency, adding a secondary reward for **Accuracy** or **Streaks** helps children slow down and think.
* **Streak Goals:** Create a reward in the Parent Dashboard for achieving a streak (e.g., getting 3 correct answers in a row).
* **Process Incentives:** Frame rewards around *improvement* or *noticing a mistake and correcting it*.

### 2. Normalize the "Explain in another way!" Feature
Getting a question wrong twice in the app is not a failure; it unlocks the **explanation block** and the AI-powered **"Explain in another way!"** button.
* Sit with your child for one session and deliberately guess incorrectly twice to "unlock" the explanation.
* Read the alternative explanation together.
* Show them that finding the explanation is a normal, helpful part of the learning cycle.

### 3. Lower the Session Length
If 20 minutes is causing your child to lose focus and start skipping towards the end:
* Go to the **Edit Profile** settings.
* Lower the sprint duration to **10 or 15 minutes**. A shorter, highly-focused session is much better than a longer session filled with bypassed questions.

### 4. Co-Regulation
If a child feels stuck on a particular topic:
* Work together on the first question of that topic.
* Encourage them to look at the **Visual Hint** (shown at the top of the question) or use the **First-Attempt Hint** to talk through the problem out loud.

## 💔 De-escalating Frustration & Resistance

If your child frequently gets cross, upset, or emotional during sessions (even when you try to walk them through it) or requires constant reminders to practice, use these proactive strategies to reset their experience:

### 1. Recognize the &quot;Amygdala Hijack&quot;
When a child is upset or crying, their brain enters a fight-or-flight threat response. The logical, calculations-based parts of their brain (the prefrontal cortex) literally go offline. 
* **The Rule:** Trying to force them to &quot;slow down, think, and write down the working&quot; in this state is biochemically impossible for them.
* **The Action:** Immediately hit **Pause** or close the app. Never force a distressed child to complete a sprint. Allow them to fully calm down first.

### 2. The 3-Minute Win (Lowering the Bar)
If your child resists starting because they anticipate a battle:
* **The Action:** Go to their profile settings and set the sprint duration to **3 minutes (Quick Test)** or **5 minutes**.
* **Why it works:** The goal is to build a habit of *success and completion* without exhaustion. A single 3-minute session completed with a positive attitude is infinitely better for their learning than a 20-minute battle.

### 3. Act as their &quot;Scribe&quot;
Completing a sprint requires several simultaneous tasks: reading the text, interpreting the problem, calculating, typing on the keyboard, and managing the timer. This creates high cognitive load.
* **The Action:** Sit with them and say: *&quot;You do the thinking, and I&apos;ll do the writing and typing.&quot;* You handle the input and timer stress, leaving them free to focus entirely on the mathematical concepts.

### 4. Create an &quot;After-Habit&quot; Routine
Needing to constantly remind or nag your child to practice creates a negative power struggle. Shift the responsibility by creating a predictable daily anchor.
* **The Action:** Pair the sprint with a pleasant, existing habit (e.g., *&quot;After breakfast, we do our 5-minute maths sprint, and then we have 30 minutes of tablet time.&quot;* or *&quot;When we get home, we do the sprint first, then have a hot chocolate.&quot;*). Once it becomes a natural part of their daily flow, reminders are rarely necessary.
